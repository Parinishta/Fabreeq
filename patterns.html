Patterns and Textures

Mixing patterns in fashion can be very challenging because you always risk looking clownish, going overboard with your look's brightness, or highlighting your body.

Including a striking pattern or print in your outfit is the simplest way to convey that you've carefully considered your attire. All repeating patterns design is called patterns, but the mixed prints fashion trend is patterns printed on fabric rather than woven or embroidered.

Rules for mixing patterns with clothes

1. Select two distinct pattern types. When pattern-mixing, allowances will mostly depend on the kinds of pieces, texture and patterns, settings, colors, and other elements in the outfit. The rule applies if you join more than two pieces with a similar design. Pretty bestsellers mixed pattern dress options can be found at the Milla store.

2. Adjust pattern scale and size. Selecting two pattern sizes—one large and one small — is usually advised. This will give the clothing depth and ensure the patterns complement one another rather than clash. While small patterns must be seen to be appreciated, large patterns are easily discerned from a distance. Take a look at the Milla collection of floral dresses, combining patterns.

3. A single bold, a single subtle. The interest level of an outfit is already increased when multiple patterns are worn, and wearing two bold patterns together is likely too much. However, they are frequently the most daring; larger patterns are only sometimes.

Texture

Texture in fashion design refers to the surface interest of a fabric, influenced by the weave and light reflection. Our eyes delight in the interplay of light on smooth or rough surfaces, while our hands can feel the textures.

Some adjectives used to describe textures are smooth, fuzzy, furry, shiny, dull, bulky, rough, crisp, heavy, and thin. The texture of the fabrics depends on many factors, like the quality of the fibers, the process used in making fabrics, or finishes applied to the fibers, yarn, or fabrics. The texture of the finished garment can be changed by different fabric manipulation techniques (ruching, smocking, trapunto, etc.).

This understanding of the dynamics of texture helps individual consumers and fashion professionals make better decisions as they select apparel products and items of personal adornment.

A Basic Guide to Textures of Different Fabrics

In the realm of fashion design, textures exhibit a range of distinctive attributes, encompassing adjectives such as smooth, rough, soft, and more. Discover the captivating variety of textures that bring depth and richness to garments.

Smooth Fabrics like silk, satin, and certain types of knits have a smooth texture that gives a luxurious and elegant feel to garments that have a sleek and polished appearance.



Rough Fabrics like denim, tweed, or burlap exhibit rough textures that add a rugged and casual element to designs.



Fuzzy Fabrics like mohair, angora, or faux fur create a fuzzy texture that adds warmth and tactile interest to garments.



Embossed Embossed textures feature raised patterns or designs pressed into the fabric surface. Embossed leather, embossed velvet, or embossed prints create a visually intriguing texture that adds depth and dimension.



Crinkled Crinkled textures have a wrinkled or crushed appearance. Fabrics like crinkled silk, crepe, seersucker, or crushed velvet exhibit crinkled textures that add visual interest and a relaxed, effortless vibe to designs.



Knit or Woven Patterns Cable knits, herringbone weaves, or jacquard patterns are those textured knits and weaves that add depth and dimension to the design.
